What is the best way to pay for a small business attorney?

Most small business lawyers charge hourly fees. After you have decided what type of lawyer you require, you can check how long it takes to finish the task. Lawyers want to form strong relationships with their clients. Lawyers may be open to negotiating a flexible fee schedule and payment plan. They might also offer alternate fee arrangements (AFAs), or work for a commission. Sometimes, small-business owners may be offered free consultations by lawyers. You can ask your questions and gain a clear understanding of the advice that attorneys might give you.

Do not let cost be the primary goal when hiring small-business lawyers. The cheapest lawyers often have less experience than the best. Although it may be tempting to choose the lowest-priced option, you might find yourself paying more. You may end up paying more over the long-term, even though it seems appealing. Either they aren't qualified or the hourly rate is higher.

Be clear with the lawyer about your expectations and budget before you hire them. Work out the fees, and then complete the standard fee agreement with the lawyer. This document states that you fill the attorney's retainer, which you should negotiate. Retainers are an agreement where the small business owner pays an advance for money done today or anytime in the future. Lawyers can pull from this fund while working on tasks for you.

Another option is a fixed price payment. Some lawyers charge a flat fee, no matter how long the task might take to finish.

Another option is to pay monthly retainers. Small businesses often choose this option if they want an experienced lawyer to be their partner for a period of time. That way, one attorney knows every part of the small business and can fix any issue that arises. After six months to a year has passed, you and the lawyer should meet and see what completed work occurred. This will allow both sides to renegotiate their monthly retainers.

Do I Really Need To Hire An Attorney?

Not all small business owners need to hire an attorney to stay protected.

Some may be able to get by with just the information found on Virginia's Business One Stop and other online resources.

Even if your entrepreneurial abilities are strong, you should consider hiring a lawyer as a first step.

It is important to assess the needs of your company and determine if hiring an attorney would be beneficial.

For example, if you're just operating as a sole proprietorship and selling jam at your local farmer's market, you probably don't need to speak with an attorney about your business.

However, if you want to expand your business across state lines (such as if you're selling products or services online), or if you want to form an LLC to protect yourself from personal liability, you may want to discuss your options with an attorney before you file any of the paperwork.

Put simply, the complexity of your business is directly proportional to your need for an attorney.

Your business will grow and you run the risk of financial and legal liability.

For this reason, it's usually wise to consult with an attorney before such problems appear, as hiring an attorney after you notice a problem will often be too little too late.

Respectful Process

The law guarantees due process. This is grounded in the principles of fairness. It simply means that you give notice to the accused and allow them an opportunity to speak. For example, a person accused of a crime must be made aware of the charges and given the chance to plead a case. More broadly, one might say that we are all entitled to know the rules of the game--say, what actions are prohibited by law--and the chance to defend ourselves.

In business, I might translate the concept as notice and opportunity to perform. Many small businesses lack job descriptions, performance metrics or evaluation reviews. This causes confusion about the type of work employees should do and whether they are succeeding. Employers are often compelled to take action before it is too late, or keep underperformers on their team for too long. Employees may sense they are off track but remain unclear about how to course correct. Other employees are blindsided altogether when they are let go. This all leads to a loss of value for both the company and its employees.

Sometimes, the absence of procedure is deliberate. I have noticed founders fear that introducing procedure will bureaucratize the business, stifle entrepreneurship, and diminish the family culture. All of these fears are reasonable and, if not addressed properly, can have serious consequences. But I've also noticed that the refusal to introduce important procedure can be equally harmful: when people are unclear about their roles or performance, they are likelier to feel demotivated, disconnected, and confused. Importantly, what serves very small startups--agility and an "all hands on deck" mentality--can be counterproductive at scale when people need clarity and organization.

Use the principle of due process to inform your employees about their job expectations, as well as opportunities for improvement. While it doesn't have to be complicated, it should be a top priority. It will improve results, raise morale, and protect both the firm and the employees.

Asking For Referrals

Attorneys are often afraid to ask anything even if it is free marketing. What about a simple review of clients? No way, that's beneath them. Referrals? They don't need to beg for work.

The thing is, you know that you can provide superior services to anyone who is referred to your office. Asking for referrals isn't asking for charity. This is asking for help to human beings. And who better to help them than you?

Attorney referral agreements and services are an interesting concept that must be treated with caution. For example, when I was a regular divorce attorney, most divorce attorneys I knew had a QDRO attorney. For every divorce case, once they finished the settlement or trial and had a judgment in hand, they would refer all of their cases to the same QDRO attorney to process the retirement account division.

What is the ABA Model Rules for this? It depends. Rule 7.2 and its comments set forth the following:

  • Referrals are free (exception token presents)
  • There are no exclusive referral arrangements. Any referral agreement must be made known to the client.
  • Referral services and lead generation services are allowed, but they cannot vouch for a lawyer's quality. The services advertised must not be misleading.

Returning to the QDRO example, such a referral practice is allowed so long as the QDRO attorney does not pay for the referrals. If the referrals are reciprocal, it cannot be exclusive, and the client needs to be kept in the loop.

Navigate Legal Structure Decisions: The Basics Of Business Formation And Conversion In Virginia

You will need to select a legal structure if you are looking to open a business in Virginia.

The default legal structure in Virginia is a sole proprietorship (or, if the business has multiple owners, a partnership).

Generally speaking, your business automatically becomes a sole proprietorship the moment you make your first sale, meaning that you must follow all relevant rules governing such businesses.

Due to the tax and liability issues that sole proprietorships can create, they are often frowned on in business.

For this reason, many small business owners choose to either start their business as a limited liability company (LLC), or convert their sole proprietorship into an LLC once it reaches a certain size.

Small business lawyers are also a valuable resource for those who wish to set up a brand and follow a specific legal framework.
